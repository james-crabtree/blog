TECH REFLECTIONS: Debugging

One thing I’ve learnt from bootcamp is that the life of a developer seems to be about 5% being confused beyond belief, 5% actually coding, and 90% debugging and fixing problems in your code. Because of that there has been A LOT of times throughout bootcamp where I’ve been working both solo and collaboratively where bugs and problems have arisen and have been needed to be addressed and fixed.


A time I encountered a bug while working solo and debugged it myself.
An example of a time I encountered something in my solo code that wasn’t working was a recent project, my personal project - which I made an online painting application for. One of the core features of the app was the ability to click on one of the different colored divs that was on top of the page and by clicking it, it would change the color of the “paint brush”.

At first I put in code that I thought was going to work and achieve the result that I wanted, safe to say that it didn’t achieve what I wanted it to and it spilled out a console full of errors. At this point I just read through each individual error message one by one and fixed them hoping it would fix it. Ultimately though, that didn’t fix it so I went over to an old friend, ChatGPT, and asked it to help me. After MANY questions and MANY different changes to the code, I still couldn’t get the feature working. Ultimately the next day I talked to a few of my course mates to get their take on it and see what they had to offer but ultimately I wasn’t able to get the feature working.


A time I encountered a bug while working in a pairing/group and debugged it collaboratively.
While I do not remember the complete exact details, when I was working in a pairing with Matt on the broken kaleidoscope challenge (the one with a bunch of colored pixels), when we first wrote our code for the pixels, the randomly colored pixels weren’t showing up and instead we were just getting a white page.

Both Matt and I first discussed the problem and provided each other with ideas on what the problem could potentially be. The next thing we did was look back through our code, immediately we went and looked at our array that we were calling to get all the different pixels. Upon doing a few different things that I believe didn’t work, we then asked for help from one of the facilitators so that we could move on and progress to the next steps in the challenge, and not fall to far behind and waste to much time trying to solve just one problem.
